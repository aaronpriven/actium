Line Numbering checklist

Aaron Priven, January 11, 2016

Assigning a line number: a checklist

This is an example of assigning an ordinary line. In all these cases, each number should only be used if it is currently available and hasn’t been used too recently.

  1. Is the line associated with a number in the underlying geography (a numbered street or highway)? Assign that number.

  2. Otherwise, determine the nearby numbers and find the used digits.

    Working this out with a chart is useful. As an example, imagine a line to be numbered is close to lines 12, 17, 36, 57, 92, 99, 653, and 805. Work out which digits are available:

2 X (12, 92)
3 X (36, 653) X (653)

5 X (57) X (805)
6 X (36)
7 X (17, 57)

9 X(92, 99) X (99)

The reversals of these lines are: 12: 21, 17: 71, 36: 63, 57: 75, 92: 29. 99, of course, is its own reverse. (Three-digit numbers don’t need to be examined for reversals.)

So 2, 6, 7, and 8 are available in the tens place, and 0, 1, 4, and 8 in the ones place. The whole list, excluding reverses, is 20, 24, 28, 40, 41, 44, 48, 70, 74, 78, 80, 81, 84, and 88. Of course in reality many of those will be used already, so the real list to choose from will be shorter. At this moment at AC Transit, the list would end up being 24, 28, 41, 44, 78, 80, 81, and 84.

  1. Is this one of the highest-ridership lines in the district? If so:

  • Assign a single-digit number not present in the one’s place on the chart (in the example: 1, 4 or 8).

  • Otherwise assign another one-digit number

  1. Assign one of the available numbers. If it is expected to have a relatively high ridership, give it a relatively memorable number (in the example, 44 or 80). If not, give it one of the other available numbers, remembering that lower numbers are considered more memorable than higher ones. For lines with very low ridership, it may be desirable to put a 1 or 2 in the hundreds place in order to preserve the lower number for lines with more ridership elsewhere.

If there are no available numbers, then one should determine which intersecting line has the least interaction with the new line to be numbered. In order, exclude:

  • Lines in the same area but that do not intersect

  • A line that just crosses at one street intersection

  • A line that meets this line at a transit center

Lines that share the same street should not have similar numbers if at all possible.

Bus Stop Naming and Announcing Protocols

The naming of stops is a difficult matter. Because stop names are used in several different contexts (in lists of a bus line’s stops, in trip plans, in on-board announcements, in service disruption notices, and more), the names must be presented in different ways. At the same time, these different presentations must still be recognizable as the same stop across these contexts.

So, there are two separate but related tasks: choosing the best name for a stop, and presenting that name in various circumstances.

Principles of naming

  • Stop names used for the public should be names that members of the public will understand. The primary responsibility in creating them is to help passengers use the transit system.

  • The name of a stop does not have to uniquely identify a stop. A stop name describes a small group of stops that are close to each other -- usually within sight – where no single bus trip stops at more than one of them, and where passengers can use signs to find the one they need. So, there are typically two stops on either side of a street intersection, both of which have the same name. In a transit center, typically all the stops will have the same name, since they are within sight of each other. A stop name is supposed to uniquely identify the area where a stop is located.

Stop name components

A stop name should be made from three different components, which, due to the varying needs of different presentations, should be stored separately.

On

This is usually the street on which the bus operates. When a stop is located within a transit center or on the grounds of an institution, the on is usually that name instead: “Eastmont Transit Center,” “Bay Fair BART,” “California School for the Deaf,” “Juan Crespi Middle School.”

At

This is usually the cross-street at which the bus stops, but the concept is a little broader than that. The at is what narrows down the larger group of stops described by the on into the smaller set of stops within sight of each other. So, for example, there are many stops on Main St., but only one or two at 1st Ave.

For some locations, such as transit centers, the name of the location fully describes the stop location, so no at is necessary.

A street address number is a kind of at, but is presented differently.

Additional text

The additional text is wording used to clarify the location of the stop for the public.

Three categories of additional text are common:

Critical Landmark. In some cases, a stop is at a street intersection, but a nearby landmark is so prominent that it outweighs that intersection in importance. In these rare cases, the landmark name should be added to the stop name. These might include train stations, transit centers, major hospitals, middle and high schools, or universities. Including landmarks in the names should be the exception, not the rule. Elementary schools, businesses, local shopping centers, building names, government buildings with few public services, places of worship, service organizations, and apartment complexes should not be included. These critical landmarks should describe the location of the stop, not merely be close to the stop.

Additional At. Sometimes, it is best to include the name of another nearby cross-street in the name, when the actual closest cross-street is minor and the other nearby cross-street is very prominent. An example would be where a major arterial road changes into a minor street at the intersection, or where a small court is closer to the stop than a major roadway. However, only when the additional street name far outweighs in importance the primary street name should it be included.

Distinguishing Text. Occasionally it is necessary to distinguish a stop from another stop in another way – one that is not so close by that it is within sight of the first. The usual example is when curvy streets intersect twice, and so a clarification such as “north intersection” or “south intersection” must be added to distinguish the stops. (For this purpose, “intersection” rather than “junction” is preferable.)

It is difficult to imagine a situation where it would be beneficial to use more than one piece of additional text. If this odd situation should in fact occur, the separate pieces should be stored separated by semicolons (“Short St.; North Berkeley BART”).

Choosing a stop name

  • The two most important factors when choosing a name for a stop are permanence and prominence. Stop names should not need to be changed often, nor should they refer to things that are not obvious to a potential passenger. However, sometimes these factors are in conflict.

  • The paths people take as they travel – streets, trails and pathways – are the primary way people navigate, and therefore their names are the best way of indicating stops. They also have names that rarely change, and streets are almost always prominently signed. Thus, most bus stops should be named for the street on which they travel and the closest cross-street. Even if a stop is a few hundred feet from the nearest intersection, as long as it is the closest stop to that intersection, the cross-street is usually the best name.

  • Street suffixes (“Avenue,” “Street,” “Boulevard,” etc.) should always be included in some presentations (such as written stop lists). In others, where brevity is crucial, it may be reasonable to omit the most common ones. However, suffixes are often important to disambiguate similarly named streets, and any removals must be carefully chosen to make sure that no ambiguities result. It is preferable to include them all, rather than to drop them by default and only put back ones that have been recognized as resolving ambiguities.

  • Only where there is no cross-street, or for some other reason using it is not desirable, then local landmarks should be considered for the “at” component of the stop name. In these rare cases where there is no cross-street, nearby landmarks of lesser importance can be considered for the name, even when those landmarks aren’t as important as the “Critical Landmarks” referred to above. For example, one would never need to add an ordinary business name to a name with a good cross-street (never “Broadway at 14th St. (Walgreens)”), but for a stop mid-block, the nearby business may be the best name available (“81st Ave. at U-Haul”).

  • Parks, government buildings or facilities, and to a lesser extent hospitals, schools, and private institutions such as places of worship and service organizations are long-lasting and prominent, so these can be good names. Note that the name of the institution itself is sufficient – it is better to use “Fire Department” instead of “Fire Department driveway,” “Main Library” instead of “Main Library entrance.”

  • Compared to these kinds of landmarks, businesses move more often and have names that change more often. Using names of businesses, even prominent ones like Walmart or Target, should be avoided if other alternatives are available. The names of business parks, shopping centers, or apartment complexes, although often not as prominent as business names, are typically longer-lasting, and should be used instead.

  • Address numbers are not as meaningful as other ways of naming stops, and should be used only as a last resort. People don’t typically navigate by address numbers until they are very close to their final destination.

  • Transit agencies, where they do not own the facility being named, honor the names used by those who do. Cities and (in unincorporated areas) counties own the streets and parks and decide on their names; train stations are named by those who own and operate them; other institutions also decide on their own names. We might justifiably disagree with the name “Warm Springs/South Fremont BART” for several reasons, but it is not our place to say.

Stop name style

  • Stop names should be in English, but when appropriate, any Unicode character should be eligible for inclusion in a stop name.

  • The importance of directional prefixes (“West Grand,” “East 14th St.”) varies locally. In some cities they distinguish completely different streets and must be included. In others, they merely distinguish address numbers from each other, and can be omitted. This varies by city and even by location within a city, so omissions should be chosen carefully.

  • If a cross-street changes names near the stop (e.g., one side of Broadway is Pleasant Valley Ave., the other side is 51st St.), the cross-street name used should be the one on the side of the street where the bus travels. (The cross-street on the other side may be added as additional text.)

  • “Opposite” should not be used in stop names.

  • Address numbers, where absolutely necessary, should be taken from the side of the street the bus is traveling, unless there is no development on that side of the street. When address numbers are used, they should always be numbers on signage visible to pedestrians near the stop. (It should be noted that the presence of midblock stops that can only be described by address numbers is often a sign that the stops are closer together than the stop spacing standards specify. The best way to deal with a hard-to-describe stop within 600 feet of the stops on either side of it may be to discontinue its use.)

  • Where appropriate, freeways shall be given with just their highway designation. AP Style uses “U.S. 101,” “I-580,” and for state highways, “Highway 24.”

  • The word “station” shall not be used in BART stations, Amtrak stations, etc. The name of the service is sufficient: “Bay Fair BART,” “Fremont Amtrak and ACE,” “Hillsdale Caltrain.”

  • Generic terms (“walkway,” “railroad crossing” should be avoided where possible.

  • We will use “Uptown Transit Center” as the on for all the stops there. Names with “Jr.” or a similar suffix will be spelled without commas: “Martin Luther King Jr. Way”.

  • “Lakeshore Avenue” and "Walavista Avenue" in Oakland will be spelled without an internal space (not “Lake Shore”). "Red Line Avenue" in Alameda will use an internal space.

  • If transit agencies share a physical stop location, it is preferred that the stop name be the same in the stop inventories of all agencies sharing that stop. This helps with regional information dissemination.

  • Non-revenue or non-public stops should not be made public.

Further clarifying information

Since several stops can have the same name, it is frequently necessary to use other information in distinguishing the stops. None of these should be part of the name, but some should often be presented along with the name.

Corner. Stops on street corners can be described as being on the “northwest corner,” “southwest corner,” and so forth, of the intersection.

Cardinal direction. Buses serving a stop are going north, south, east, or west (or some intermediate direction like northeast).

City. An exception to the rule that stops with the same name should be within sight of each other is when stops in different cities are at intersections with identically named streets or institutions (e.g., Bret Harte Middle School). In circumstances where ambiguity is possible, the city should be presented along with the stop name.

Site (near side / far side). Although useful inside the transit agency, this information is jargon and should never be used for the public. Its main use in public information is that if corner and site are both provided, the cardinal direction can be determined.

Bay number. This distinguishes stops within transit centers. Unless the transit center is very big and stops are very far away, for the public it is usually better to not use bay numbers, and instead rely on the lines that serve the stop to distinguish one stop from another. Note that an important exception is the Salesforce Transit Center, which is indeed very big and the stops are not all within sight of each other, even on the same level.

Stop Code. A public identifying number for the stop within the transit system. (This field is named for the GTFS term “stop code,” which is different than an internal-to-agencies-only “stop ID”).

Clarification. Occasionally some additional comment is helpful to distinguish a stop from nearby stops or make it easier to find, e.g., “on traffic island” or “underneath overpass.” These should not be part of the stop name, but might be presented in some circumstances, e.g., a trip plan. These are usually not necessary, however, since for the transit agency, the geolocation should be sufficient to find the stop.

Presentation

Each of these components should be stored as separate database fields and made available to data consumers for reassembly in the way that is best for each application. This could be done by adding fields to the GTFS stops.txt file, or by making the names available separately. However, since most data applications will not be customized to use this detail, it is still important to provide good defaults for these applications.

General presentation principles

  • Text should follow conventional rules of English spelling, grammar, and writing style, including punctuation. Technical limitations may require in some cases abbreviating information, but this should be a last resort and should be worked around as much as possible. Rather than thinking of stop names as data, they should be thought of as writing, and need to be written properly, just as with any other passenger communication.

  • Names of streets and other components are proper nouns and should be appropriately capitalized, with the first letters of important words in capitals. Mixed case is easier to read than all-capitals text. Names that use irregular capitalizations in ordinary writing (e.g., “MacArthur”) should use the same capitalizations in presentations of stop names. Where generic labels must be used in stop names, they should be in lower case (“pathway” or “entrance”).

  • In writing, numbers shall be given as digits: “1st St.”, “135th Ave.” Ordinal suffixes (“st”, “nd”, “th”) should not be printed in superscript.

  • In writing, the following abbreviations should be used for street endings: “Ave.” for Avenue, “Blvd.” for Boulevard, “Cir.” for Circle, “Ct.” for Court, “Dr.” for Drive, “Pl.” for Place, “St.” for Street, and “Terr.” for Terrace. Other street names should be spelled out.

  • Other abbreviations should be used in specific circumstances: "Mt." for "Mount" at the beginning of "Mt. Eden" or other similar place names, and "Jr." when used as part of someone's name.

GTFS: stop_name and stop_desc

GTFS provides two fields designed to provide human-readable stop information.

[Required]The stop_name field contains the name of a stop, station, or station entrance. Please use a name that people will understand in the local and tourist vernacular.

[Optional] The stop_desc field contains a description of a stop. Please provide useful, quality information. Do not simply duplicate the name of the stop.

Components should be assembled to form these two fields. The stop_name should be assembled as follows:

<On> at <At> (<Additional Text>)

Except that a stop with a street address number should be assembled this way:

<Street Address Number> <On> (<Additional Text>)

The stop_desc should be assembled as follows:

<Corner> corner, going <cardinal direction>. Bay <Bay number>. <Clarification>.

No stops should have all these fields present, and the spaces or punctuation around fields that aren’t actually present should be omitted. Directions should be spelled out as words (“northeast,” “south”) and not abbreviated.

The following abbreviations should be used: “Ave.” for Avenue, “Blvd.” for Boulevard, “Cir.” for Circle, “Ct.” for Court, “Dr.” for Drive, “Pl.” for Place, “St.” for Street, and “Terr.” for Terrace.

Hastus name

The Hastus stop name should match the GTFS stop_name, except:

  • Due to the limited number of characters in the Hastus stop name, the colon should continue to be used to separate the On and At components.

  • When field length or character set limitations in Hastus prevent the correct stop name from being used, a close approximation should be created and used instead.

Audible on-board announcements

Some announcements on board buses are required by the Americans with Disabilities Act, but announcements are helpful for all passengers and the ADA requirements should be viewed as a minimum, not a maximum. Announcing every stop is helpful for all passengers. At the same time, too much noise on board buses gives an unpleasant riding experience. Thus, announcements should not have any information beyond that needed to identify the stop. Every additional piece of information, added in an attempt to be helpful, adds to the cacophony on board an inherently noisy vehicle.

  • The announcement system does not need to announce the first stop on a line, unless passengers are expected to ride through the layover, as on loops.

  • The announcement system should not announce stops that should not be used for alighting (such as stops heading toward San Francisco on Transbay lines that do not accept local passengers).

  • If the name of a stop is an address, it should be read as one: “500 International Blvd.”, not “International Blvd. at 500” or the like.

  • Otherwise, for the second and subsequent stops, it should announce the on only if it changes from the previous location (even if that location was the first stop and not announced). If the street changes, it should first announce the on followed by the word “at.”

  • For all stops that are announced, the system should announce the at, and where present, the additional text. Street suffixes should be announced wherever possible, and any words that are typically abbreviated in print should be read out in full. Say “Avenue” and not “Ave,” for example.

  • It should be “Cal State” and “Cal State East Bay” (not spelled out C-S-U).

  • Freeway numbers should be rarely used, but when used, should be said as follows: “Interstate Eighty”, “Interstate Two Thirty-Eight”, “Interstate Five Eighty”, “Interstate Eight Eighty, ” etc. “U.S. One Oh One.” For California state highways: “Highway Thirteen”, “Highway Twenty-Four,” “Highway Two Thirty-Seven,” etc. (Highway numbers for non-freeways should not be used, and street names such as “San Pablo Ave.” or “Mission Blvd.” used instead.)

  • Although the “hundred” in highway numbers should not be pronounced, it should be pronounced in numbered avenues: “One Hundred Fourth Avenue.”

  • “End of line” should be announced after the stop at the end of the line, if all passengers are expected to alight.

Lists of a bus line’s stops

Each stop in a the list of stops of a bus line should be presented in the same way as the GTFS stop_name, above. In addition, the stop_code should be presented alongside the name, and the city should also be listed, either along with each name or as a subheading. So either:

Oakland

Martin Luther King Jr. Way at 61st St. (55127)

Berkeley

Adeline St. at Alcatraz Ave. (52252)
Martin Luther King Jr. Way at Prince St. (Ashby BART) (52177)

Or:

Martin Luther King Jr. Way at 61st St., Oakland (55127)
Adeline St. at Alcatraz Ave., Berkeley (52252)
Martin Luther King Jr. Way at Prince St. (Ashby BART), Berkeley (52177)

Depending on the presentation, the stop code could also be separated from the name in a separate column, or using other punctuation than parentheses.

Sign location

New schedule signs or flags have locations printed on them, partially for customer use, but mainly to make it possible for the maintenance crew to install them. Because of this, they should include the site information not usually included in public information. The general pattern is “<On> at <At> (<Additional Text>), <City>, <Site>, <Cardinal Direction>. (Stop <stop_code>)”

So:

Fremont Blvd. at Thornton Ave., Fremont, near side, going east. (Stop 52523)

Written materials

Textual descriptions of stops should generally include the on, at, and additional text. However, these require presentation flexibility. Sometimes, it will be best to combine multiple stops on the same street into a single description (“Stops on Broadway at 38th St., 40th St. and 42nd St.”). Since text descriptions are usually ephemeral, it can be better to use the name of local businesses as the most prominent landmark (“Broadway at 14th St., in front of Walgreens”) rather than the cardinal direction or other ways of clarifying where the stop is. In any event, clarity and comprehensibility must be our aim: our deep and singular aim.
